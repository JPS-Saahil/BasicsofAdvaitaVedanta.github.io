Part One

The Seer and the Seen

Drig Drishya Viveka

Vedanta begins with a simple observation that most people overlook their entire lives:

In every experience, there are always two things: the one who sees, and the thing being seen.

This is not a metaphor. It is a structural fact about all experience. Follow the question downward:

Whatever can be observed cannot be the observer.

Start at the outermost level. A pot, a tree, a sound, or the body is seen by the eye. At this level, the object is the seen, the eye is the seer relative to that object, and the two are different. The seen is many and changing; the seer at that level remains distinct from it.

Move inward once. You know whether your eyes are clear, weak, tired, open, closed, healthy, or unwell. So the eye itself has now become the seen, and the mind is the seer relative to the eye. Again the three principles hold: the seer and the seen are different, the seen are many while the seer is one, and the seen changes while the seer does not.

Move inward again. The mind is also seen. Thoughts, emotions, memories, doubts, and decisions keep changing. So the mind becomes the seen, and the sākṣī becomes the seer relative to the mind. The mind is many and changing, while the sākṣī is one and unchanging. That sākṣī is the Atman, and in the nondual teaching the Atman is Brahman.

Three principles of the seer and the seen
1
Seer and the seen are different

The pot, chair, tree, or sound is seen by the eye. The eye is therefore the seer at this level, while the object is the seen. They are different: the seen are many, and the seen changes while the seer remains one for that level.

Example “I see the book,” “I see the chair,” “I hear the bell”: the object is known, and the knower stands apart from it.
2
Seen are many, seer is one

You also know whether the eyes are clear, weak, tired, open, closed, healthy, or unwell. So the eye becomes the seen, and the mind becomes the seer at the next level. The seen are many in their changing conditions, while the seer is one knower of those conditions.

Example One mind can know many eye states: clear sight, blurred sight, fatigue, blinking, strain, or rest. The eye is seen; the mind is the seer relative to the eye.
3
Seen changes, seer doesn't

The mind itself is seen as changing. Thoughts arise and subside, emotions appear and fade, memory comes and goes, and decisions shift. So the mind becomes the seen, and the sākṣī becomes the seer at the deepest inward level. The seen changes; the sākṣī does not.

Sākṣī The witness is like the light that reveals a room. The objects in the room may change, but the light does not become any of them. In the same way, the sākṣī remains untouched as Atman, and Atman is Brahman.

From object to eye to mind to witness: the object is seen by the eye, the eye is known by the mind, and the mind is known by the witness. At each stage, the seer and the seen are different, the seen are many, and the seen changes, while the seer remains one and steady.

Rūpaṃ dṛśyaṃ locanaṃ dṛk
Tad dṛśyaṃ dṛk tu mānasam
Dṛśyā dhīvṛttayaḥ sākṣī
Dṛg eva na tu dṛśyate
Forms are seen by the eyes.
Eyes are known by the mind.
Thoughts are known by the Witness.
The Witness itself is never seen.
Clay and pot. A pot is made of clay. If you break the pot, only the clay remains. From the clay’s point of view, there was never a pot at all, only clay.

Satyam. Everything is not Brahman; alone the Brahman is.

Brahma satyaṃ jagan mithyā
Jīvo brahmaiva nāparaḥ
Brahman alone is real; the world is mithyā; the individual self is none other than Brahman.
Part Two

Who Are You?

Having followed the ladder to its end, Vedanta asks directly: What do you actually identify as?

Most people answer: "I am my body," or "I am my thoughts," or "I am my personality." Vedanta says: look again.

Before going further, pause on something most people say without questioning:

Sometimes you say - "I am in this body."
Other times you say - "This body is mine."

If you are the body - then who is the one inside it? If the body is yours - then who is the one owning it? A thing cannot own itself.

When you say "my hand," you are separating the hand from the one saying "my." When you say "my mind," you are placing yourself apart from the mind. So who is the one saying my - to the body, to the mind, to the emotions, to the entire life?

Clouds are always changing - stormy, calm, thick, thin. The sky does not move. It holds all of it without being disturbed by any of it.

Your thoughts change constantly. Your emotions rise and fall. But here is something worth sitting with: you were aware of that change the entire time. That knowing - that silent awareness watching the changes - was never itself changing.

Śaṅkarācārya first shows why you are not the body, and then why you are not the mind. The body is many-parted, ruled, visible, impure, dark, and temporary. The Self is one, partless, the inner ruler, pure consciousness, luminous, and eternal.

Part Three

Ātman - The True Self

What the Witness really is

This witnessing awareness, recognised as your true nature, is called Ātman. It is not your name, personality, memory, body, or ego. It is the pure witness - present before the first thought this morning, present after the last tonight. It was never born. It does not age. It cannot suffer.

Śloka 5 Nature of Ātman
Nodeti nāstam etyeṣā na vṛddhiṃ yāti na kṣayam ।
Svayaṃ vibhāty athānyāni bhāsayetsādhanam vinā ॥
This (Consciousness) does not rise, nor does it set.
It does not increase; nor does it suffer decay.
Being self-luminous, it illumines everything else - without any other aid.
What this means: The sun needs no lamp to be seen. Ātman needs no instrument - no eye, no mind - to be aware. Every act of knowing borrows its light from Ātman. The knower is never itself an object of knowledge, because the moment you make it an object, something else is already knowing it. That "something else" is the Witness.

Śloka 4 Ātman as the Illuminator of the Mind
Kāmaḥ saṅkalpasandehau śraddhāśraddhā dhṛtītare ।
Hrīr dhīr bhīr ityevamādīn bhāsayatyekadhā citiḥ ॥
Desire, determination and doubt, belief and non-belief,

Why this matters: Every mental state - fear, desire, doubt, courage - is an object to something. You can watch fear arise. You can notice doubt. That which watches all these without being any one of them is Ātman, the Consciousness that is a unity. It never changes as the states change.

Whatever can be observed is not the observer. The Ātman is the observer that was never observed.

Part Four

Brahman - Universal Consciousness

The ground in which everything appears

Now Vedanta pushes further. Is this awareness yours - private, locked inside your skull? Or is awareness itself something universal - one thing appearing through many forms?

Śloka 14 What Creation Really Is
Sṛṣṭirnāma brahmarūpe saccidānandhavastuni ।
Ambhau phenādivatsarvanāmarūpaprasāraṇā ॥
Creation means the manifesting of all names and forms
in the entity which is Existence-Consciousness-Bliss - Brahman -
like the appearance of foam and waves in the ocean.
The analogy: Foam is not separate from the ocean. Waves are not a second substance added to water. They are water, appearing as foam and wave. In the same way, every name and form - every person, mountain, thought - is Brahman appearing as those things. Brahman is both the material and the ground.

The electricity analogy: each bulb differs in shape and wattage; the light looks separate. But the electricity powering all of them is one and the same current. Vedanta says: the awareness in you and the awareness in every being is not similar - it is the same awareness, appearing through different forms.

Śloka 20 Five Aspects - Three Belong to Brahman
Asti bhāti priyaṃ rūpaṃ nāmacety aṃśapañcakam ।
Ādyatrayaṃ brahmarūpaṃ jagadrūpaṃ tato dvayam ॥
Existence, Luminosity, Lovableness, Form, Name -
these five aspects belong to every entity.
The first three are of the nature of Brahman;
the next two are of the nature of the world.
AstiExistence (Sat) - it is. The existence of a pot is not the pot's property; when the pot breaks, existence remains.
BhātiLuminosity (Chit) - it shines, is known. The light by which all things are known is Consciousness itself.
PriyamLovableness (Ānanda) - you do not love objects; you love the fullness that appears when the mind quiets. That fullness is your own nature.
Nāma · RūpaName and Form - these belong to Māyā. They appear and disappear; what remains constant is Sat-Chit-Ānanda.
Śloka 21 Brahman is the Common Ground in All Things
Khavāyvagnijalorvīṣu devatiryannarottamaḥ ।
Abhinnāssaccidānandāḥ bhedyante rūpanāmani ॥
In ether, air, fire, water and earth -
in gods, animals and humans -
Existence, Consciousness and Bliss are equally present as common features.
Names and forms make one differ from the other.
The insight: Strip away the label and the shape from any object and what remains? Pure existence - which is Brahman. This is not a belief; it is a direct observation waiting to be made. Stand before a gold shop: you see rings, chains, bracelets - hundreds of objects. A goldsmith sees one thing: gold. Vedanta trains the goldsmith's eye.

The Upaniṣads distill this into four great statements. Each one is an entire teaching.

These are not affirmations to repeat. They are descriptions of what is already true, waiting to be recognised.

How Ātman Equals Brahman - Step by Step
1
Ātman is pure Consciousness

The Witness is aware without any instrument. It does not use the eyes, the mind, or the intellect - these are its objects. It is simply aware. This awareness is what philosophers call "pure" - free of all content, yet illumining all content.

Śloka 5: "Svayaṃ vibhāty athānyāni bhāsayet sādhanam vinā" - It shines of itself and illumines all else without any aid.
↓
2
Brahman is pure Consciousness

Brahman is described as Sat (Existence), Chit (Consciousness), Ānanda (Bliss). Chit - Consciousness - is its very nature, not a property it happens to have. Brahman does not possess awareness; it is awareness.

Śloka 14: "Brahmarūpe saccidānandhavastuni" - in the entity that is Existence-Consciousness-Bliss which is Brahman.
↓
3
There can only be one undivided Consciousness

If Ātman is pure Consciousness and Brahman is pure Consciousness, are they two? Only if Consciousness could be divided - but space cannot be divided by drawing a line in it. The line appears; the space remains one. The apparent division of awareness into "your" awareness and "universal" awareness is a division in name and form only, not in the substance itself.

Śloka 21: Existence-Consciousness-Bliss is equally present in ether, fire, water, gods and humans alike. Only names and forms differ.

∴
Ātman = Brahman - Ayam Ātmā Brahma

The witnessing awareness you call "I" and the universal ground called Brahman are the same undivided Consciousness. The difference was never real - it was a difference of name and form, not of substance. This is what Tat Tvam Asi points at: You are That. Not "you will become That after much effort" - but You are That, right now, as you read this.

The seeing and the liberation are not two events. They are one.

Take any object - a simple cup of tea. It exists (asti). You know it is there (bhāti). There is something pleasant about it (priyam). It has a name (nāma) and a form (rūpa). This applies to everything.

Asti = Sat (Pure Being). The existence of the cup does not belong to the cup. When the cup breaks, existence does not break. That pure existence, present in all things, is Brahman.

Bhāti = Chit (Pure Consciousness). The cup is known - but what makes it knowable? Not your eyes; the eyes are also objects. The light by which all things are known is Consciousness itself.

Priyam = Ānanda (Pure Bliss). You do not love objects. You love the peace and fullness that temporarily appears when the mind quiets around an object. That fullness is your own nature, momentarily recognized.

Stand in front of a gold shop. You see rings, chains, bracelets - hundreds of different objects. But a jeweler looks at the same display and sees one thing: gold. Vedanta trains you to be the jeweler.

Part Six

Māyā

The power that hides what is real and projects what is not

If Brahman is everywhere - why do we feel separate, limited, and lost? If awareness is our very nature - why do we spend our lives chasing things to feel complete?

Walking at dusk, you see a snake in the road. Your heart races. You freeze. Then someone shines a light: it is a rope. The snake was never there. But the fear was completely real - the trembling hands, the racing heart, the paralysis. All of it was real. The cause alone was false.

This is exactly what Vedanta says about the human condition. Brahman is the rope. The individual life - with all its fear, seeking, and suffering - is the snake. It is real as an experience. It is not real as an independent substance.

Māyā is the name for the power that makes the rope appear as a snake - and keeps it appearing even when you have been told it is a rope.

The DDV identifies Māyā as having two aspects - not as two separate forces, but as two sides of one coin. They arise simultaneously and cannot be conceived apart from each other:

Āvaraṇa Śakti
The Power of Concealment - Veiling
Māyā hides the truth of Brahman. It is not that Brahman disappears - the sun does not vanish when clouds cover it. But from where you stand, the clouds are all you see. Āvaraṇa is the veil that makes you not-know your own nature. It does not create ignorance in Brahman - Brahman is untouched. It creates the appearance of ignorance in the Jīva, the seemingly individual self. This concealment operates both subjectively (you do not recognise the Sākṣī as your true nature) and cosmically (Brahman appears to be divided into a world of separate things).
Effect: "I am this body. I am limited. I am mortal. I am this name and history."
Vikṣepa Śakti
The Power of Projection - Creating
Having concealed the truth, Māyā does not leave a blank. Into the space of that concealment, it projects a substitute - the entire world of names and forms, of ego and object, of birth, suffering, death, and rebirth. The DDV (Śloka 13) says: Vikṣepaśaktir liṅgādibrahmaṇḍāntaṃ jagat sṛjet - "the projecting power creates everything from the subtle body to the gross universe." This projection is not malicious. It is the nature of the power. A lamp casts shadows wherever light is blocked. Māyā projects wherever Āvaraṇa veils.
Effect: Ego, attachment, multiplicity, competition, desire, grief, the entire drama of samsāra.

Think of a cinema screen. The screen is on fire - you are not alarmed, because you know it is a projection. But imagine you forgot there was a screen. You would experience real terror. You might run. You might warn others. The projection would become completely real to you.

Āvaraṇa: forgetting the screen. Vikṣepa: taking the movie as real. Vedanta is not about destroying the movie - the world remains, activity continues. It is about remembering the screen. And the screen was never touched by anything that appeared on it.

What Māyā Is - and Is Not

Māyā is one of the most misunderstood words in Vedanta. People hear "illusion" and think it means "nothing exists" or "the world is fake." This is wrong. Vedanta is precise:

Māyā is not "nothing"
The dream you had last night was not "nothing." It was vivid, emotional, felt completely real. But it was not real in the way the waking world is. Māyā is like that dream - real as an appearance, not real as an independent substance. The world has empirical reality. You pay your bills. You love people. These matter. But they are not the ultimate, unchanging, independent reality that Brahman is.
Māyā is not separate from Brahman
Māyā is said to reside in Brahman - as the power of fire to burn resides in fire. It is not an external enemy attacking Brahman from outside. Brahman is untouched by it. The DDV (Śloka 35): Brahmaṇyavasthitā māyā vikṣepāvṛttirūpiṇī - "Māyā, characterised by projecting and concealing, rests in Brahman." The power is real; the effect (apparent division) is not ultimately so.
Māyā cannot be described as real or unreal
Māyā is called anirvacanīya - indescribable. It is not real, because it does not ultimately exist as a separate thing. It is not unreal, because it produces real effects (the snake-fear was real). It is neither and both - it belongs to a category that does not fit the ordinary pair. This is why Vedanta says: do not try to explain Māyā fully. Instead, recognise its effects and go beyond it.
The Question Māyā Raises
If Brahman is alone, complete, without lack - why does Māyā exist at all? Why would the infinite project an illusion of limitation?

Vedanta does not answer this fully - because the question itself is asked from within Māyā. Asking "why Māyā?" is like a character in a dream asking why the dream exists. The question dissolves at awakening, not before. Every answer you could give would itself be a product of Māyā - so no answer from within the dream can reach outside it.

Seen more plainly: if nothing is missing in Brahman, where would limitation come from? Limitation is not a second reality competing with Brahman. It is an appearance that belongs to the realm of experience, not to the absolute. The snake appears in the rope - but the rope itself never became a snake.

Now use the snake image carefully: the poison of a snake affects what is other than the snake. The snake itself is not harmed by its own venom. In the same way, Māyā has no power over Brahman. It becomes effective only where there is a mistaken sense of being separate from Brahman. This is why liberation is not Brahman escaping Māyā. It is the Jīva recognising that it was never separate from Brahman - and the venom, finding no host that is truly other, loses its power entirely.

Part Seven

Three Levels of Reality

Vedanta does not say the world does not exist. It says reality is graded. What is absolutely real never changes. What is practical works within experience. What is illusory appears for a time and is later corrected. Understanding this difference is one of the most precise and liberating insights in Vedanta.

Pāramārthika Satya
Absolute Reality
Brahman alone - without a second, without beginning or end, without change. This is the only level at which something exists completely, unconditionally, and without depending on anything else. It cannot be cancelled by any other experience. It was never created. It will never end. This is the standard against which all other claims to reality are measured.
Vyāvahārika Satya
Practical / Transactional Reality
The world you live in - bodies, objects, relationships, karma, language, society. This level is real and fully functional within the waking state. You pay your bills. You grieve. You love. These are not dismissed. But this reality is dependent - it exists relative to a perceiving mind, within a particular state of consciousness. It has not always been here and it will not always be. When the waking state ends, the waking world disappears with it - just as a dream city vanishes when you wake.
Prātibhāsika Satya
Apparent / Illusory Reality
Objects that appear real but are cancelled even within the same experience - the rope seen as a snake in dim light, the desert mirage, the dream world while dreaming. These are real long enough to produce real responses (fear, thirst, joy) but are contradicted without needing to leave the level of experience. They serve as a model for understanding the nature of the waking world itself from a higher standpoint.

Here is the uncomfortable question Vedanta asks: from whose standpoint is the waking world real?

Within the dream, the dream world is fully real. The objects, the people, the events - all completely convincing. There is fear, joy, pain. Only upon waking does the dreamer say: "none of that was truly real. I was the only one there." The entire drama - every character, every crisis - was a projection of the single dreaming mind.

Vedanta says: what you call the waking world stands in exactly the same relation to Brahman as a dream stands to the dreamer. Every body, every mountain, every civilization - a projection within the one Consciousness. We are not merely living in a world. We are living in the dream of Brahman.

This is not nihilism. The dream is not meaningless - it has its own internal coherence, its own beauty, its own moral weight. But it is not ultimate. And the one who knows this does not flee the dream. They simply stop being lost in it. They live fully, but lightly - as one who knows they are dreaming and moves through the dream with wonder rather than fear.

We are living in the dream of Brahman.

Part Eight

Three Bodies, Three States

We are not just a physical form. Vedanta identifies three bodies, each operating at a different level:

Sthūla Śarīra
The gross body - physical form, senses, and action. It is the visible layer of experience, born, changing, and dying in time.
Sūkṣma Śarīra
The subtle body - prāṇamaya, manomaya, and vijñānamaya kośas. It carries breath, thought, feeling, memory, and the sense of individuality.
Kāraṇa Śarīra
The causal body - the seed condition in which saṃskāras and vāsanās remain hidden before they unfold into thought, desire, and action.
Sūkṣma Śarīra in detail:Prāṇamaya kośa is the life-force sheath, carried by the five prāṇas - prāṇa (inward breath), apāna (downward flow), samāna (balancing / digestion), udāna (upward flow / speech), and vyāna (circulation). Manomaya kośa is the mind sheath of feeling, doubt, memory, and imagination. Vijñānamaya kośa is the discerning sheath that judges, understands, and decides.

Saṃskāras are the impressions left by repeated actions and experiences. Vāsanās are the latent tendencies that grow from those impressions and quietly shape habit, desire, and thought.

Both saṃskāras and vāsanās are stored in the Kāraṇa Śarīra and persist across lifetimes. Liberation involves not suppressing them but recognising they belong to the body-mind - not to the witness aware of them.

Vedanta also identifies three states of experience - and a fourth that is not a state at all:

Turīya
The Witness of All States

Not a fourth state - the silent awareness that underlies and witnesses the three. The Māṇḍūkya Upaniṣad calls it turīya - "the fourth" - but it is not a state you enter and exit. It is what you are. Always present. Never in any state, yet the ground of all states. It does not come and go with waking, dream, or sleep. It is the screen that all three states appear on. Destroy the film and the screen remains, exactly as it was.

Jāgrat
Waking
Gross body active, outer world experienced through the senses. The Māṇḍūkya Up. calls the waking self Viśva - the one who experiences the gross world outwardly.
Svapna
Dream
Subtle body active - mind creates its own world from the inside. The dream self is called Taijasa. Every object, person and event in the dream is made entirely of your own mind. Feels completely real while it lasts.
Suṣupti
Deep Sleep
No ego, no world, no thoughts - yet something was present to register the peace. The self here is called Prājña. All duality dissolves. What remains is the bliss of your own nature, unobstructed - though not yet recognised as such.
Understanding the Three States - Deeply

The Māṇḍūkya Upaniṣad (the shortest of all Upaniṣads - only 12 verses - yet considered the most complete) opens by saying: Sarvam hy etad brahma - "All this is indeed Brahman." It then maps this through the three states and Turīya, showing that Brahman is present in and as each one.

Jāgrat - The Waking State (Gross Experience)

This is where you spend most of your life. The gross body is active. The five sense organs take in the world. The mind interprets. The intellect decides. The ego claims: this is happening to me. The Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad (4.3.11) distinguishes the waking self from what it experiences, noting that you move between waking and sleep - which means neither is permanently you. The waking world, vivid as it is, exists only while the waking state lasts. In deep sleep, every city, every relationship, every problem - completely gone. Not reduced. Gone. This is the first clue that the waking world is not the absolute.

Svapna - The Dream State (Subtle Experience)

In dream, the gross body is completely inactive - yet you are fully present. You see, hear, feel, fear, love. The Chāndogya Upaniṣad (8.10.1) points out that a dreaming person experiences things that have no external basis whatsoever - mountains, people, dangers - all built entirely by the mind. The DDV (Śloka 11) describes it precisely: "the inner organ, identifying itself with the reflection of Consciousness, imagines ideas in the dream." The dream feels as real as waking - until waking dissolves it instantly. This parallel between dreaming and waking is one of Vedanta's most pointed teachings: the waking world too, seen from a higher standpoint, is a kind of collective dream - projected by the universal mind rather than the individual one.

Suṣupti - Deep Sleep (Causal Experience)

This is the most philosophically interesting state - and the most overlooked. In deep sleep: no thoughts, no ego, no world, no suffering, no desire. Complete cessation of all ordinary experience. Yet when you wake, you say: "I slept deeply. I knew nothing. It was peaceful." Who registered that peace? Who knew the absence of knowing? Something was present. The Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad (4.3.21-22) is explicit: in deep sleep, the self shines by its own light - the light of the Witness - and though ignorance veils the world, the witnessing awareness remains. The DDV (Śloka 10) says: "In the state of deep sleep, when the thought of ego disappears, the body also becomes unconscious." The ego vanishes. The body is inert. Yet you wake as you - not as someone else. The continuity is the Witness.

A Transactional Example - One Person, Three States, One Night

Consider Arjun - a 38-year-old CFO. He is respected at work, worried about a board presentation tomorrow, proud of his car, anxious about his daughter's health, and quietly certain that he is in control of his life. He goes to bed.

Waking state (10 pm): Arjun lies in bed reviewing the board presentation. His mind runs through numbers, anticipates questions, worries about a rival who might undermine him. The "I" - Arjun the CFO, the father, the worried man - is fully operative. The whole architecture of his identity is intact. This is Jāgrat.

Dream state (midnight): Arjun dreams. He is in a forest - not an office - running from something. The board, his daughter, his car - none of them exist in this dream. He has a different body, a different setting, new fears. Yet he is still "I." The dream-I is as convinced of its reality as the waking-I was an hour ago. The mind has created an entire world from its own material - and the Chidābhāsa (reflected consciousness) lights it all up. When Arjun wakes at 3 am and remembers the dream, he will call it unreal. But while it was happening, it was completely real to him. This is Svapna.

Deep sleep (3-6 am): Arjun falls into dreamless sleep. The CFO vanishes. The daughter-worry vanishes. The boardroom vanishes. The dream-forest vanishes. There is nothing. No Arjun. No fear. No ambition. No identity at all. The ego is suspended in the causal body like a seed. All the saṃskāras rest dormant. Yet when he wakes at 6 am and his wife asks how he slept, he says: "Deeply. I knew nothing. It was peaceful." He has reported on an experience. But there was no Arjun present to have the experience. Who reported the peace? This is the question Suṣupti poses. This is Suṣupti.

The constant: Through all three - the CFO worried about numbers, the dream-man running in a forest, the blank of deep sleep - one thing was present without interruption. Not Arjun. Not any particular identity. The simple fact of awareness - the Witness - which never entered any of the three states, yet made all three possible. That is Turīya. That is Ātman. That is what Arjun, and you, and every being actually is - beneath every state, every role, every fear, every name.

The DDV's point: you move between all three states - waking, dream, sleep - every single day. You are none of them. You are the one who passes through all three unchanged. Recognise that, and the root of suffering is cut.

Part Nine

Vṛtti - How the Mind Knows

Easy way: sun → plate → dark room → object → knowledge

Vṛtti is the shape the mind takes when it meets an object. In this teaching, the key idea is simple: the mind does not know by itself; it becomes ready to know when consciousness illumines it.

The sun, the plate, and the dark room
01
The sun is the witness. It stands for pure consciousness, which is always shining and never changes.
02
The shining plate is reflected consciousness. The plate does not create light on its own; it only reflects the sun’s light.
03
The dark room is the mind. Inside it are many objects, just like thoughts, memories, and sense impressions are already present in the mind.
04
The object becomes visible. When the reflected light reaches the object, the object is known. In the same way, when attention turns toward an object, the mind takes that form and knowledge appears.
So the sun is consciousness, the plate is the mind reflecting consciousness, the dark room is the mind-space with objects, and the visible object is what is finally known.
Vṛtti-vyāpti and Phala-vyāpti
A
Vṛtti-vyāpti. The mind reaches the object and takes its shape. This is like the reflected light entering the dark room and falling on the object.
B
Phala-vyāpti. Consciousness illumines that mind-form, so the object becomes known. This is the final “I know this” moment.
Put simply: vṛtti is the mind-form, vṛtti-vyāpti is the mind reaching the object, and phala-vyāpti is knowledge arising when consciousness lights it up.
An Analogy

The Lamp, the Pot, and the Knowing

Imagine a dark room with a clay pot resting on the floor. The pot is there, but until light falls on it, it is not known. A lamp is brought into the room, and the pot becomes visible.

The lamp does not become the pot. It simply reveals the pot. In the same way, when the mind meets an object, it takes the shape of that object. That shaped thought is the vṛtti.

When consciousness illumines that vṛtti, knowledge appears. The mind has gone to the object, the object has taken a form in the mind, and consciousness makes that form known. That is vṛtti in action.

Lamp = consciousness that reveals
Pot = the object to be known
Dark room = the mind that has not yet taken the object’s form
Vṛtti = the mind shaped by the object

So the pot is not known because the lamp becomes the pot. It is known because the lamp shines on the pot. In the same way, the object is known because consciousness shines on the vṛtti.

An Analogy

The Sun, the Bucket, and the Reflection

Imagine a clear morning. The sun rises and floods the world with light. Every place is touched by that light, yet not every place shows the sun in the same way. This is Brahman: one, whole, self-luminous, and present everywhere.

Now imagine three buckets of water placed in a field under the same sun. One bucket is full, still, and clear. One is half-full and slightly muddy. One is almost empty and stirred by the wind.

In each bucket, a reflection of the sun appears. Three suns - or so it seems. Each reflection is different: one bright and sharp, one dim and distorted, one flickering and broken. And yet there is only one sun. The sun itself has not divided. It has not moved. It has not changed. The differences belong entirely to the water.

Sun   =   Brahman - one, self-luminous, undivided
Bucket   =   the individual body-mind (Jīva)
Water   =   the mind (antaḥkaraṇa)
Reflection   =   Chidābhāsa - the ego, the individual sense of "I am aware"

The quality of the reflection depends entirely on the water. A still, clear mind reflects the light of consciousness sharply - the Jīva seems almost transparent, nearly Self-like. A turbulent, murky mind distorts the reflection - the ego seems dense, confused, very far from Brahman.

But here is what the analogy reveals: the sun never entered the bucket. It was never wet. It never became a small sun. The reflection is real as a reflection - but it is not a second sun. When you tilt the bucket and the reflection vanishes, you have not destroyed the sun. You have only ended the appearance that was never the original.

This is why Vedanta says you do not need to create the Self or reach Brahman. Brahman is already shining. The work is only to still the water - to quiet the mind - until the reflection becomes clear enough for you to recognise: that which is reflecting and that which is reflected are not two things. The light in the bucket is the light of the sun. The awareness in the Jīva is the awareness that is Brahman.

Part Nine

Ego - The Reflected Self

Vedanta uses a very careful distinction here. The true witness is the light of awareness itself, while the ego is only a reflection of that light in the mind.

Sākṣī
The witness - pure awareness, unchanged by body, thought, or emotion.
Chidābhāsa
Reflected consciousness - the seeming individual “I” that borrows its light from the witness.
Ahaṃkāra
The “I-maker” - the function that says “I think,” “I feel,” “I act.”
Easy way to see it: the sun is not in the bucket, but its reflection appears on the water. The reflection looks like a sun, yet it depends completely on the real sun. In the same way, ego looks like a self, but it is only consciousness reflected in the mind.
Reflected Self and True Witness
Ego
I feel like the doer, the thinker, the one in charge.
Witness
That sense of “I” appears only when consciousness is reflected in the mind.
Ego
So am I the witness?
Witness
No. The witness is what knows the ego. What is known cannot be the knower.
Ego
Then what am I really?
Witness
You are the awareness in which the reflected “I” appears and disappears.

So the ego is not a separate entity with its own power. It is a useful but mistaken identity created when the mind reflects consciousness and then claims that reflected light as “me.” Liberation begins when this confusion is seen clearly.

Part Ten

Why We Suffer

The root is not sin. It is not bad luck. It is not fate.

The root is misidentification - adhyāsa. Taking the reflection for the original. Taking the wave for the ocean. Taking the name-and-form for the whole.

Fill a pot. The space inside seems separate from the space outside. Break the pot. The space does not merge - because it was never actually divided. Your body is the pot. The awareness inside is what you call "yours." Brahman is the infinite space. The death of the body does not release your awareness into Brahman - because it was never actually separate from Brahman.

Part Twelve

Mokṣa - Liberation

Not an arrival. A recognition.

Mokṣa is the most misunderstood word in Vedanta. People imagine it as a destination reached after death, a blissful trance, a reward for austerity, or something acquired through long practice. The Drig-Drishya Viveka corrects all of this directly.

Mokṣa is the permanent removal of the false identification that made you believe you were ever bound.

You are not trying to become Brahman. You already are Brahman. The work is to stop insisting - through every thought, habit, and assumption - that you are something less.

Bhidyate hṛdayagranthiḥ
Chidyante sarvasaṃśayāḥ
Kṣīyante cāsya karmāṇi
Tasmin dṛṣṭe parāvare
By beholding Him who is high and low -
the fetters of the heart are broken,
all doubts are dissolved,
and all karmas wear away.
- Drig-Drishya Viveka, Sloka 31 (from Muṇḍaka Upaniṣad 2.2.8)

Three things happen simultaneously at the moment of recognition. The heart-knot - the identification of awareness with the ego - is severed. All doubts about the nature of the Self dissolve, because the questioner and the answer were never two things. And karma loses its binding force: accumulated and future karma are burned; only prārabdha, the karma already in motion like an arrow released, continues until this body ends.

The Three Identifications That Bind - and How They End

The Drig-Drishya Viveka identifies three ways the ego (Ahaṃkāra) falsely identifies itself. Each has a different mechanism of release:

Natural (Sahaja)
Ego ↔ Reflected Consciousness
The moment the ego and the reflection of consciousness come into existence together, they identify with each other. This bond cannot be broken by effort - it dissolves automatically when the Jīva fully realizes its identity with Brahman. The ego disappears into Brahman like a wave absorbed by the ocean.
Due to Karma (Karmaja)
Ego ↔ Body
The ego identifies with a particular body according to past karma. This identification ends naturally when prārabdha is exhausted - when the body completes its appointed course. This is why even one who has realized Brahman continues to live in a body until the karma sustaining that body is spent.
Due to Ignorance (Bhrānti)
Ego ↔ Witness (Sākṣī)
The ego mistakes itself for the Witness - it says "I am aware" and believes that "I" is a limited thing. This is destroyed only by Knowledge (jñāna). Once the real nature of consciousness is seen clearly, this superimposition cannot persist. This is the root identification; removing it is liberation itself.
The three disappear together. When the Jīva fully realizes itself as Brahman, all three identifications dissolve simultaneously - like removing a single thread that was holding a knot, and the entire knot falls apart at once.

What Liberation Actually Looks Like

The body continues. The world continues. Prārabdha runs its course - the Jñānī may still experience hunger, cold, grief in the body's dimension. But here is the precise distinction the book makes:

Before: consciousness identified with the ego - looking outward at the world as an object it must secure, fear, or possess.
After: consciousness recognizes itself as the ground of the ego, the world, and all experience - like the screen recognizing it was never affected by the fire in the movie.
The wave does not vanish. But it stops believing it is separate from the ocean.

The Drig-Drishya Viveka uses a strikingly beautiful image for what happens after sustained practice becomes second nature:

Dehābhimāne galite vijñāte paramātmani
Yatra yatra mano yāti
Tatra tatra samādhayaḥ
With the disappearance of attachment to the body
and with the realization of the Supreme Self -
wherever the mind goes,
there, Samādhi is.
- Drig-Drishya Viveka, Sloka 30

This is the state the book calls sahaja samādhi - natural, effortless absorption. It is not a trance. It is the ordinary moment, fully awake, in which every object is seen as Brahman, because nothing else exists to see.

The Three Jīvas and Who Is Liberated

The book offers a precise threefold analysis of the individual self - and it matters for understanding liberation:

Prātibhāsika Jīva
The Illusory Self of Dream
The self that appears in dream - completely convincing while it lasts, dissolved upon waking. It was never real. Its world was only the dreaming mind. Liberation does not apply here; this self is already unreal and requires no release.
Vyāvahārika Jīva
The Empirical Self of Waking Life
The self you take yourself to be - the one with a name, a history, desires, fears, and karma. This self has empirical existence from beginningless time until liberation. It is not real in the absolute sense, nor is it unreal - it operates functionally. Liberation for this self means dissolving into the Sākṣī, like waves dissolving into water.
Pāramārthika Jīva
The Real Self - Already Free
The Sākṣī - the pure Witness that is identical with Brahman. This self was never bound. It never entered the dream, never took a body, never suffered. It is what you have always been. Liberation is not something that happens to this self. It is this self being recognized as what was always the case.
The paradox of liberation: the self that is bound is not real. The self that is real was never bound. So liberation is not an event that happens to anyone - it is the recognition that the apparent bondage was an appearance, like a snake seen in a rope in dim light. When light comes, the snake does not run away. It was never there.

What the Scriptures Say

The Drig-Drishya Viveka ends by gathering several Upaniṣadic declarations of the fruit of recognition:

Brahma veda brahmaiva bhavati
He who knows Brahman verily becomes Brahman - Muṇḍaka Up. 3.2.9
Tarati śokam ātmavit
The knower of Self goes beyond grief - Chāndogya Up. 7.1.3

Knowing it, one goes beyond death - Śvetāśvatara Up. 3.8

Verily all that exists is Brahman. Foam, wave, and water were never truly separate. Ego, world, and Brahman are equally non-separate. Mokṣa is simply the clear seeing of what always already was.

Part Thirteen

Samādhi - The Six-Fold Map of Meditation

From the Drig-Drishya Viveka, Slokas 22-29

The Drig-Drishya Viveka is unusual among Vedantic texts for its detailed, systematic account of meditation. It does not describe Samādhi as one thing. It identifies six distinct forms - three inward, three outward - each operating at a different depth, each appropriate to a different stage of the practitioner.

The principle behind all six: having understood intellectually that "That Thou Art" - that Jīva and Brahman are one - the practitioner must now strengthen that conviction until it becomes direct, unshakeable, and spontaneous. The instruction: upeḥya nāmarūpe dve saccidānandatatparaḥ - samādhiṃ sarvadā kuryād hṛdaye vā'thavā bahiḥ - "having become indifferent to name and form, devoted to Satchidānanda, one should always practise concentration - either within the heart or outside."
Upeḥya nāmarūpe dve saccidānandatatparaḥ
Samādhiṃ sarvadā kuryād hṛdaye vā'thavā bahiḥ
Having become indifferent to name and form,
devoted to Existence-Consciousness-Bliss -
one should always practise concentration,
either within the heart or outside.
- Drig-Drishya Viveka, Sloka 22

Why name and form? Because names and forms are impermanent - they appear and disappear. Even in deep sleep they are absent. What remains constant in every object, in every state, is Existence (asti), Consciousness (bhāti), and Bliss (priyam) - and these belong to Brahman, not to Maya. The practitioner learns to rest in what is constant, and become indifferent to what changes.

The Two Streams - Six Forms

Samādhi

The practitioner turns inward and takes their own mental states - desire, doubt, fear, joy - as objects of observation. Rather than being swept away by them, awareness watches each one. Then Consciousness is meditated upon as the silent Witness of all these states. This is the first settling of the mind toward its own source.

The support shifts to sacred affirmation. The practitioner holds the words: "I am Existence-Consciousness-Bliss, unattached, self-luminous, free from duality." The text gives this directly as the content of this Samādhi. The sound is not mere repetition - it is a precise description of the real Self, held until meaning ripens from intellectual understanding into direct recognition. Knower, knowledge, and known are still three - hence "with ideas" (savikalpa).

Without thought construct · Sloka 26
All supports are released - no object, no affirmation, no subtle inner arrangement. The mind becomes like a flame in a windless place: steady, unflickering, utterly still. The practitioner is completely absorbed in the bliss of recognizing the Self, indifferent to both perceived objects and verbal ideas. → Do nothing. Rest as awareness. Let everything be as it is.
Different from deep sleep
In deep sleep there is no knowledge of Self. In Nirvikalpa Samādhi there is no feeling of not knowing the Self - the practitioner is identified with the ever-conscious Ātman. Deep sleep is ignorance; Nirvikalpa is pure knowing beyond subject-object division.

The uninterrupted flow of this reflection is itself the Samādhi. Sloka 28: Akhaṇḍaikarasaṃ vastu saccidānandalaḥṣaṇam - ityāvicchinnacinteyaṃ samādhirmadhyamo bhavet.

The book instructs: "The practitioner should uninterruptedly spend time in these six kinds of Samādhi." In this state the practitioner is so fully present to Brahman that ordinary sense-pulls lose their traction entirely.
The bliss of this state
The insensibility to external objects is not unconsciousness - it is the mind being so fully absorbed in Brahman that nothing else draws it. Like a person so absorbed in conversation that they do not hear the noise around them. The absence is not vacancy but fullness.

Sustained practice of all six forms, over a long time and with intensity, leads to a state that is neither inward nor outward, neither with support nor without - because the recognition has become the natural texture of every moment.

Swami Nikhilananda · Māṇḍūkya Upaniṣad Commentary

Waking and Dreaming: Are They Different?

Ten objections tested, and each one answered
Source frame

Nikhilananda keeps the practical value of waking life, but he does not let practice become proof of ultimate truth. The test is bādha, or sublation. What a higher seeing cancels cannot be final.

Waking belongs to daily, working reality. Dream belongs to private appearing reality. Both are known in consciousness. Turīya is the ground in which both are seen.

Practical level
Waking matters for work, relationships, and ethics. The text never denies that.
Test of truth
Shared experience, memory, and the body-mind system show consistency, not the absolute.
Final verdict
Both waking and dreaming are appearances in consciousness. Neither is Brahman.

In his landmark translation and commentary on the Māṇḍūkya Upaniṣad, Swami Nikhilananda first gives the strongest case for the difference between waking and dreaming, and then tests each argument. The point is simple: at the level of ultimate analysis, waking and dreaming belong to the same order of appearance. Both are experienced states in consciousness, and neither is absolute.

Understanding why they seem different, and why that seeming is unreliable, is the foundation of the Māṇḍūkya's non-dual vision.

Why does this matter? If waking feels more real because it is shared and useful, Vedanta asks a deeper question: shared with what, useful for whom, and steady from which standpoint? Consciousness is present in waking and dream alike, so the deepest test is not practicality alone, but the ground that knows both.
The Common View
The waking world is real and solid. The dream world is fleeting and private. They are fundamentally different kinds of experience, and conflating them is philosophically naive.
The Vedāntic Challenge
Every feature you use to privilege waking over dreaming, when examined carefully, is either absent in certain waking experiences, present in certain dream experiences, or relies on circular reasoning. The difference is practical - not absolute.
01
Objection - The Argument for Difference
The waking world is publicly shared; the dream world is private and solitary.
The Argument
When you sit in a room with others, every person present perceives the same table, the same walls, the same light. You can verify your perceptions against theirs. The dream, by contrast, is entirely private. No one else enters your dream. No one can confirm what you saw. This shared, intersubjective character of waking experience is what makes it objective and real in a way the dream cannot be.
The Refutation
The test of sharedness cannot be applied from within the dream. While you are dreaming, the dream feels equally shared and external - dream-others appear to confirm your perceptions. You only know the dream was private after waking. More critically: the waking world is shared only from the standpoint of waking consciousness. From the standpoint of Turīya - the ground beneath all states - even the waking world is a kind of collective projection. The number of minds sharing a projection does not determine whether the projection is ultimately real. Many people shared the same dream does not make it the Absolute.
∴ Sharedness is a feature within experience, not a measure of ultimate reality.
02
Objection - The Argument for Difference
Waking objects have causal efficacy - they do things and produce lasting effects. Dream objects do not.
The Argument
If a waking fire burns your hand, a real blister forms. If you eat waking food, your hunger is genuinely satisfied. The effects persist and can be traced. Dream fire does not produce real burns. Dream food does not nourish. The waking world interacts causally with the body in a continuous, verifiable way. The dream does not. Causal power is the mark of reality.
The Refutation
Dream objects do produce real effects - on the body. People wake from nightmares with racing hearts, sweat, elevated blood pressure, and genuine hormonal changes. A person who dreams of falling wakes in a startle with muscle contractions. Fear dreamed is fear felt in the body. Moreover, within the dream, causal chains are perfectly consistent - dream fire burns, dream hunger is satisfied, dream injuries produce dream pain. The causal chains only appear broken when you change the frame of reference to waking. But this is circular: you are presupposing that waking is the standard, which is precisely what is under dispute.
∴ Causal efficacy exists in both states relative to their own frame. The frame-change that reveals dream-causation as unreal is itself an act of consciousness - and that act of consciousness is what Vedanta investigates.
03
Objection - The Argument for Difference
The waking world is spatially and temporally consistent and ordered; the dream is chaotic and incoherent.
The Argument
In waking life, space feels continuous and law-governed. You travel from one place to another and the world stays consistent. Time flows in one direction. The past remains past, the future has not yet arrived. In dreams, scenes shift quickly, people can change shape, time can slow down or jump, and logic can break apart. Coherence, order, and lawfulness seem to mark the real.
The Refutation
Order and incoherence are both features of experience, not ontological grades. While dreaming, the dream appears fully ordered and coherent. Only after waking do you classify it as chaotic. A lucid dreamer, present inside the dream with waking-level awareness, often finds the dream perfectly orderly. Furthermore, certain waking experiences - psychosis, high fever, deep grief, or trauma - produce experiences as incoherent as any dream. Does this make those waking experiences unreal? The consistency of the waking world is guaranteed by the stability of the instruments of perception (senses + brain). A different and equally stable instrument - the dreaming mind - produces its own form of consistency. Stability of appearance is not the same as ontological ultimacy.
∴ Order is a feature of the apparatus of perception. A stable apparatus produces stable appearances. Stability of appearances is not a proof of absolute reality.
04
Objection - The Argument for Difference
Waking experience is verified by all five senses simultaneously; dream experience involves only internal mental imagery.
The Argument
When you pick up a mango in waking life, you see it with your eyes, smell it, feel its texture and weight with your hands, taste it, and possibly hear it when you bite. Five independent channels of input converge on the same object, providing multi-layered confirmation. In dreaming, there is no external object at all - the brain is simply generating all the data internally, without any external stimulus. Multi-sensory corroboration is the hallmark of objective reality.
The Refutation
Multi-sensory experience can occur in the absence of an external object. Hallucinations - which are clinically documented, cross-cultural, and neurologically understood - present full multi-sensory vividness with no external basis whatsoever. Dreams themselves can involve all five apparent senses simultaneously: a detailed dream landscape has visual panorama, sound, touch, smell, and even taste. More philosophically: the senses do not contact objects directly. They generate neural signals, which the mind interprets. The mind is the final synthesizing organ of all sensory data. If the mind generates the same signals - whether from an external stimulus or from within - the resulting experience is indistinguishable. Even in waking life, you are not touching the table directly; your nervous system is constructing the experience of touching. The external object is inferred, not directly known.
∴ All sensory experience is processed by the mind. The mind's construction of experience is the constant. The presence or absence of an external cause for that construction does not determine the felt reality of the experience itself.
05
Objection - The Argument for Difference
Waking experience can be independently verified by instruments and third parties; the dream cannot.
The Argument
If I say there is a star 500 light years away, multiple telescopes in multiple countries can verify this. Photography, measurement, and independent instruments converge. If I say I dreamed of a purple mountain, no instrument, no photograph, no second person can confirm this. Empirical verification through reproducible, instrument-supported, third-party observable processes is the foundation of all scientific knowledge of the real.
The Refutation
Instruments verify the consistency of the waking-world framework, not its absolute reality. A telescope is a physical object whose output is read by a human mind. The entire chain - photon, mirror, eyepiece, retina, optic nerve, visual cortex, mind - is still a mind-mediated process. Instruments verify cross-referencing within a shared framework. But the very framework being used - physical, measurable, extended space - is what is under examination. You cannot use a tool that belongs to the framework as proof that the framework is absolutely real, any more than you could prove a dream was real by pointing to other things in the same dream. Within the dream, other dream-instruments would equally "verify" dream-objects. The self-consistency of a system is not proof of its absolute reality.
∴ Verification is always verification within a framework. No instrument reaches beyond its own framework. The question Vedanta asks operates prior to all frameworks.
06
Objection - The Argument for Difference
Waking experience is continuous and remembered; dreams are fragmentary and quickly forgotten.
The Argument
You wake up this morning as the same person who went to sleep last night, and the same person who existed years ago. Your waking memories are continuous, reliable, and long-lasting. Dreams, by contrast, evaporate within minutes of waking. Most people forget 90% of their dreams. The fragility and transience of dream memory compared to waking memory confirms that dreams lack the ontological stability that waking experience possesses.
The Refutation
Continuity of memory is a function of the recording instrument, not a measure of what was real. A person under anaesthesia cannot remember the surgery - this does not mean the surgery did not happen. The continuity of the waking self is maintained by the waking-state brain and its memory systems. These systems are inactive during REM sleep, which is why dreams are not reliably stored. But the experience during the dream was fully real while it occurred - the fear, the joy, the vividness. What is being argued is that the waking state's recording apparatus is more durable, not that the waking state's objects are more real. A photograph that fades does not retroactively make the moment it captured unreal. Further: the waking self that appears continuous is itself periodically dissolved - in deep sleep, in anaesthesia, in certain meditative states. If continuity of memory were the measure of reality, the waking self would also fail, since there are nightly gaps.
∴ Memory durability reflects the stability of the recording mechanism, not the ontological weight of what was recorded. The waking self, too, has gaps in its continuity.
07
Objection - The Argument for Difference
The waking world exists independently of whether any particular person is perceiving it; dreams vanish when the dreamer wakes.
The Argument
The table in your living room was there before you woke up, exists while you are away, and will be there after you die. Its existence does not depend on you perceiving it. Dream objects, by contrast, have no existence independent of the dreamer. The dream mountain does not continue to exist when you wake up. Perception-independence is the classic mark of objective reality.
The Refutation
The claim that waking objects exist independently of perception is itself an inference made from within waking consciousness. You have never actually verified the table's existence when no mind is present - because the moment a mind verifies it, a mind is present. The perception-independence of physical objects is a philosophical assumption, not an observed fact. Modern quantum physics has made this even murkier: at the quantum level, observation appears to be constitutive of measurement outcomes, not merely passive recording. More critically: within the dream, dream objects also appear to exist independently of your direct attention. You turn away from the dream-mountain, and when you look back, it is still there. The appearance of perception-independence is identical in both states. The discovery that the dream ceases when you wake is made after the fact, from outside the dream. We cannot know whether the waking world similarly ceases when there is no consciousness of any kind to perceive it.
∴ Perception-independence is inferred from within waking, never directly observed. The same inference operates within the dream. Both states present the appearance of observer-independent objects.
08
Objection - The Argument for Difference
Actions taken in waking life have moral and karmic weight; dream actions do not.
The Argument
If you help someone in waking life, you accumulate good karma. If you harm someone, the karmic consequence is real. Scripture, dharmaśāstra, and ordinary moral reasoning all hold that waking actions matter morally. Dream actions - killing in a dream, loving in a dream - have no moral weight. No tradition holds a person morally responsible for what they did in a dream. This moral asymmetry confirms the ontological asymmetry.
The Refutation
The moral asymmetry is acknowledged and is entirely consistent with the Vedāntic analysis - but it does not resolve the question of ultimate reality. Vedanta's three-tier framework explicitly places waking life in the vyāvahārika (practical/transactional) realm and dream in the prātibhāsika (apparently real) realm. This difference in tier means that waking karma matters, waking ethics matter, waking relationships matter - all within the transactional order. Vedanta does not deny this. But the question of moral consequence operates at the empirical level. The philosophical question is: is either level ultimately real in the sense that Brahman is ultimate? Neither is. Both are appearances in the same ground consciousness. Karma operating in the waking state is real within vyāvahārika satya - just as the rules of a dream are real within that dream. Neither cancels the other's appearance. Both dissolve in Brahman.
∴ Moral weight operates at the transactional level. Vedanta confirms it there. The question of ultimate reality is prior to and independent of questions of moral consequence.
09
Objection - The Argument for Difference
In the waking state, the subject (perceiver) and the object (perceived) are truly separate. In the dream, the subject and object are both made of the same mind-stuff - so there is no genuine object at all.
The Argument
When you see a stone in waking life, you and the stone are genuinely different substances. The stone is extended, inert matter. You are a conscious being. The duality is real. In the dream, however, the dream-stone and the dreaming-you are both made of the same mental substance - they are both modifications of one mind. The dream-stone has no existence outside your mind. So in the dream, the subject-object distinction is fake. In waking, it is real. This makes the waking world structurally superior.
The Refutation
This argument proves too much - it actually supports the Advaita position. If you grant that in the dream, perceiver and perceived are both modifications of one mind, you have already demonstrated that subject-object duality can be a complete appearance without being an ultimate reality. The dreamer experiences genuine duality - stone vs self - yet you and I agree it is made of one substance. Now Vedanta asks: what makes you certain that the same analysis does not apply to waking life? In Advaita, both the waking self (Jīva) and the waking world (jagat) are said to be modifications of the one Brahman - just as dream-self and dream-world are modifications of one dreaming mind. The very structure of the argument you made to dismiss the dream is precisely the structure Vedanta uses to understand the waking world.
∴ The demonstration that dream duality is apparent duality within one substance is precisely the model Vedanta applies to waking duality. The argument against dreaming, fully followed, leads straight to Advaita.
10
Objection - The Argument for Difference
No one, not even a philosopher, acts on the belief that waking life is a dream. This practical indistinguishability proves that waking is privileged.
The Argument
Whatever philosophers may argue, people everywhere treat waking life as serious. They plan, work, avoid danger, care for others, and respond to the world as if it matters. This universal practical response across humanity seems to show that waking life is objectively privileged.
The Refutation
Vedanta never asks you to stop living. The practical priority of the waking world is fully acknowledged and preserved within daily life. Fire burns, food nourishes, kindness matters, and responsibilities remain real at the working level. But that is not the same as ultimate reality. A shared appearance proves shared experience, not final truth. We all see the sun rise and set, but that appearance does not prove the sun circles the earth.
∴ Vedanta fully preserves and validates practical waking reality. The inquiry is not an instruction to behave as though waking life is a dream - it is an investigation into what grounds all experience, waking and dreaming alike. Universal practical behavior at the empirical level is not a philosophical proof of absolute ontological status.
The conclusion that follows

Having dismantled all ten arguments, Swami Nikhilananda arrives at the same shore as the Māṇḍūkya Upaniṣad itself: no feature of the waking world, when examined without presupposition, proves that it is ontologically superior to the dream world as a whole-cloth independent reality. Both states are appearances within consciousness. Both arise. Both subside. Both are real within their own framework. Neither is Brahman.

This is not scepticism or nihilism. It is the precise, surgical application of the same standard to both states - and the discovery that what remains when both are seen as appearances is the only thing that was never an appearance: the witnessing awareness itself.

Śaṅkara's Guru · Gauḍapāda · Māṇḍūkya Kārikā

Ajātivāda: The Doctrine of Non-Origination

Nothing was ever born. Nothing ever happened. There is only Brahman.
Source frame

Ajātivāda takes the earlier analysis to its limit. If waking and dream are both appearances in consciousness, then origination itself cannot be final. Gauḍapāda therefore denies real creation, real bondage, and real liberation at the absolute level.

The point is simple. If an effect is never truly separate from its cause, then nothing has genuinely been born. The teaching is not meant to deny daily life. It is meant to stop us from mistaking appearance for origination.

Non-origination
No real birth, no real death, no real becoming at the absolute level.
Firebrand image
The wheel appears while motion continues. When motion stops, the wheel is seen to have never been separate from the firebrand.
Not nihilism
Empirical life remains valid for ethics and practice, even while the highest truth denies ultimate origination.

Ajātivāda - ajāta (unborn) + vāda (doctrine) - is the most radical philosophical position in all of Advaita Vedanta. It was given its systematic formulation by Gauḍapāda, the paramaguru of Śaṅkarācārya (Śaṅkara's teacher's teacher), in his monumental work the Māṇḍūkya Kārikā (also called Gauḍapādīya Kārikā), a verse commentary on the Māṇḍūkya Upaniṣad composed around the 7th century CE.

Within the Advaita tradition, Śaṅkara is often understood as treating the Māṇḍūkya as especially direct and liberating, and Gauḍapāda's Ajātivāda stands as the most radical expression of that non-dual standpoint. Śaṅkarācārya himself honored Ajātivāda as the highest teaching, calling the Māṇḍūkya alone sufficient for liberation - and Gauḍapāda's Ajātivāda is the metaphysical backbone of that declaration.

The Central Claim
Brahman alone is. Nothing truly became anything else. No creation ever occurred. No individual ever arose. What looks like life is a līlā, a play of names, forms, mind, and consciousness. It seems real in daily life, but it is not absolute.
What appears as the world, the individual, birth, suffering, and seeking is lived through mind and consciousness. In Advaita language, it is mithyā: not absolutely real, not absolutely unreal, but a superimposition on the one truth that is Brahman. The superimposition never truly disturbed Brahman, because nothing ever truly happened to Brahman at all.
How Ajātivāda relates to Śaṅkara's Vivartavāda: Śaṅkara presents Vivartavāda as apparent transformation. The world seems to arise from Brahman the way a rope seems to become a snake. Gauḍapāda's Ajātivāda goes further. It says even the appearance of origination has no final standing. Nothing was ever truly transformed. Śaṅkara used Vivartavāda as a teaching step, and honoured Ajātivāda as the highest statement of the truth.

Gauḍapāda organizes his teaching across four chapters of the Māṇḍūkya Kārikā. The pillars of Ajātivāda can be understood as follows:

I
The Impossibility of Origination
Māṇḍūkya Kārikā II.48, III.48, IV.3–7

Gauḍapāda begins by asking a simple question about origination itself. For something to come into being, it must arise from either:

(a) Something real - but if the cause is already real and complete, what need has it to produce an effect? Brahman, being infinite and without lack, has no motive to produce anything. Causation requires a deficit in the cause, which Brahman cannot have.

(b) Something unreal - but the unreal cannot produce the real. A barren woman's son cannot build a house. Nothing cannot give rise to something.

(c) Itself - but a thing cannot be both cause and effect of itself simultaneously. This is circular.

(d) Something other than itself - but if Brahman is non-dual, there is no "other" to be a cause.

Every logical avenue leads to the same conclusion: origination is logically incoherent. Not merely unprovable, but self-contradictory. What appears to arise has never truly arisen at all.

Ajāyamāno bahudhā vijāyate - "The unborn appears to be born in many ways." What is unborn does not become born; it only appears to. The appearance itself is the mystery of Māyā. But the appearance has no standing in ultimate truth.
II
The Dream-Waking Analogy - Taken to Its Limit
Māṇḍūkya Kārikā II - Vaitathyaprakaraṇa (Chapter on Unreality)

The second chapter of the Kārikā is called Vaitathyaprakaraṇa - the chapter on unreality (vaitathya = falsity, untruth). Gauḍapāda deploys the dream analogy not merely to understand the three states, but to systematically demonstrate that the waking world is ontologically equal to the dream world.

His argument: the waking world and the dream world are equally unreal because both appear only to the perceiving mind, both are absent in deep sleep, and both are contradicted by a higher state of consciousness (Turīya). The waking world is not cancelled by waking up from a dream - but it is cancelled by the recognition of Turīya. And what can be cancelled is not absolute.

This is a precise logical move. In classical Indian epistemology, bādha (sublation, cancellation) is the test of reality. A lower cognition is cancelled by a higher one. The snake is sublated by the rope, revealing it was never real. Similarly, both waking and dreaming are sublated by the recognition of Turīya - which means neither was ultimately real.

Svapnavac ca - "Just as in a dream." The objects of the waking world are, like dream objects, mind-born, not self-subsisting. The only difference is the type of mind that dreams them: the individual dreamer dreams the personal dream; the cosmic mind (hiraṇyagarbha) dreams the waking world. Scale and stability differ. Reality does not.

Gauḍapāda also adds the observation that the spatial and temporal dimensions within which waking objects appear are themselves mind-born. Space and time are frameworks of experience, not containers into which Brahman is poured. Strip them away, and there is nothing left to be "born" or "to die" in.

III
Advaita - The Non-Arising of Multiplicity
Māṇḍūkya Kārikā III - Advaitaprakaraṇa (Chapter on Non-Duality)

The third chapter is the Advaitaprakaraṇa, where Gauḍapāda presents the most careful analysis of the nature of jīva - the individual self. His central point: the jīva was never truly born as a separate individual entity. The appearance of individuality is a superimposition on the one Brahman, comparable to the appearance of the snake on the rope.

Key verse (III.48): Na nirodho na cotpattir na baddho na ca sādhakaḥ / na mumukṣur na vai mukto ity eṣā paramārthatā - "There is no dissolution, no creation, no one in bondage, no one who is a seeker, no one who desires liberation, and no one who is liberated. This is the absolute truth."

Na nirodho na cotpattir na baddho na ca sādhakaḥ - "No dissolution, no creation, no bondage, no seeker, no one desiring freedom, no one who is free. This is the ultimate truth." - Gauḍapāda, Māṇḍūkya Kārikā III.48

This verse is one of the most quoted and most philosophically consequential in the entire Advaita tradition. It says: from the ultimate standpoint, the drama of spiritual seeking - the aspirant, the path, the bondage, the liberation - is as much a superimposition as the world it is trying to escape. The seeker and the sought are both appearances in the one Brahman that was never disturbed.

This does not mean that spiritual practice has no value. Gauḍapāda is careful: from the empirical, practical standpoint (vyāvahārika), the path is real, the seeker is real, karma is real, and ethics are real. His radical claim is the ultimate view - the view from Turīya - and it cannot be arrived at by bypassing the empirical path. But once the truth is seen, it is seen that there was never anything to transcend.

IV
Alātaśānti - The Extinguishing of the Firebrand
Māṇḍūkya Kārikā IV - Alātaśāntiprakaraṇa (Chapter on Quenching the Firebrand)

The fourth chapter takes its name from a vivid analogy. Imagine a firebrand - a burning stick - being whirled rapidly in the dark. It appears to trace a circle of fire, straight lines, curves, and intricate patterns. None of these shapes actually exist. They are optical appearances created by the movement of a single point of fire. When the movement stops, the patterns vanish. They were never there.

The firebrand is consciousness. The apparent patterns are the world of names and forms, the cycle of birth and death, the individual and its karma. None of the shapes ever existed as independent realities. They were movements - vibrations - of the one consciousness, creating appearances that have no substance of their own.

Alātacakravad dṛśyate - "It appears like the wheel of a firebrand." When the firebrand stops moving, there is no wheel. There never was a wheel. There was only the firebrand. When the movement of Māyā ceases - when the mind becomes perfectly still - the world of appearances dissolves, and only Brahman, which was always there, remains visible as what always was.

This chapter also engages with Buddhist philosophy, and scholars debate the extent to which Gauḍapāda was influenced by Madhyamaka and Vijñānavāda Buddhist thought. Gauḍapāda himself is clear that his position is rooted in the Upaniṣads - specifically in the Māṇḍūkya's teaching that Turīya is not a state but the ground of all states. Where Buddhism denies the permanent self (anātman), Gauḍapāda affirms the one self that is Brahman - he refuses the void, affirming instead the fullness that appears empty only from within the dream of multiplicity.

The result of this teaching - alātaśānti, the quenching of the firebrand - is not extinction but recognition. The movement stops. The patterns dissolve. What remains is not nothing. What remains is the pure, self-luminous, ever-present Brahman-Ātman: ajam, advaitam, turīyam - unborn, non-dual, the fourth.

Why Ajātivāda Is Not Nihilism - And What It Actually Means to Live It
Gauḍapāda and Śaṅkara - The Practical and the Ultimate

The most common misunderstanding of Ajātivāda is that it makes life meaningless. If nothing ever arose, why do anything? Why be compassionate, why practice, why care? Gauḍapāda and Śaṅkara address this directly.

The empirical world is not denied. It is accurately categorised. Ajātivāda does not say the world of suffering is unreal the way a mirage is unreal and can be dismissed. It says the world of suffering has no independent existence apart from Brahman - but within its own level, the suffering is as real as anything can be, and compassion, ethics, and practice are not only valid but essential. The path is real at the empirical level. Only after the path is walked does the walker discover that they were always already at the destination.

Brahmaivedam amṛtaṃ purastad brahma paścād brahma dakṣiṇataś cottareṇa / adhaś cordhvam ca prasṛtaṃ brahmaivedam viśvam idaṃ variṣṭham - "Brahman alone is this immortal. Before it is Brahman, behind it is Brahman. To the right and to the left it is Brahman. Below and above it extends. This whole world is Brahman alone - the highest." - Muṇḍaka Upaniṣad 2.2.11

For the one who recognises this - for the jīvanmukta, the liberated while living - there is no contradiction. They act fully in the world, respond to suffering with complete compassion, fulfill their duties, and engage in life with total presence. But they are not bound by the outcomes. The wave moves, but it knows it is the ocean. The firebrand may still glow, but it knows there was never a wheel.

Ajātivāda's deepest gift is this: it removes the last trace of effort from the concept of liberation. You are not trying to reach something you do not have. You are not trying to escape something that has real hold on you. You are recognising what has always been true - that the Brahman you seek is the Brahman that is seeking, and the search and the searcher and the sought were all along one movement of the one that never moved at all.

Na nirodho na cotpattir
na baddho na ca sādhakaḥ ।
na mumukṣur na vai mukto
ity eṣā paramārthatā ॥
There is no dissolution, no origination,
no one in bondage, no one who is a practitioner,
no one who desires liberation, no one who is liberated -

- Gauḍapāda, Māṇḍūkya Kārikā III.48
The lineage that carries this teaching: Gauḍapāda → Govindapāda (Śaṅkara's direct teacher) → Ādi Śaṅkarācārya. Śaṅkara called the Māṇḍūkya Upaniṣad the crown of all Upaniṣads, capable of giving liberation by itself. Gauḍapāda's Kārikā is the philosophical instrument that unlocks it. Together they form the foundation on which all subsequent Advaita Vedanta stands - and Ajātivāda is their most uncompromising conclusion: that truth was never hidden, nothing was ever broken, and the one that is reading these words is the one that was never born and will never die.
